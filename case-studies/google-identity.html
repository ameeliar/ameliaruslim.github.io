Timeline

Four years of identity design at Google

From my first enrollment launch to the design frameworks still in use today – a chronological view of what I shipped, proposed, and influenced across Advanced Protection, Security Settings, and Enhanced Safe Browsing. Collectively, these represent systematic friction reduction across the identity and security surface, each demonstrating a specific skill – scoping, cross-team alignment, advocacy, pattern reuse – while methodically chipping away at the program's accessibility problem from every angle available.

Shipped January 2020

"One-click" enrollment

I launched a phone-based enrollment flow that spiked enrollments 12x – but 72% of newly enrolled users were getting locked out, and we ultimately deprecated it.

Six months after joining the team, I launched the "one-click" enrollment flow – the first version of Advanced Protection that didn't require purchasing physical security keys. Users could enroll with a phone-based "built-in" security key, launched natively on Android and via the redesigned Smart Lock app on iOS.

Enrollments increased by 12x within two weeks, surpassing a long-awaited 50k threshold with 60k enrollments one month post-launch. But we quickly discovered that 72% of newly enrolled users were getting locked out of their accounts – the enrollment removed friction but didn't adequately prepare users for the authentication requirements that followed. We deprecated the flow three months later.

This failure was instructive. It proved that lowering the barrier could drive adoption, but that enrollment and ongoing usability had to be designed together. The lockout problem became the central design constraint for everything that followed.

During this period I also designed the enrollment path for iOS users via the Smart Lock app, advocating for completing enrollment on the user's iPhone rather than forcing multiple device switches. ~50% of users directed to Smart Lock successfully proceeded with enrollment.

2021 | The strategic case for a different model

The shipped improvements were meaningful, but I recognized they had a ceiling. As long as Advanced Protection required hardware keys, it would remain a niche program protecting a fraction of the people who needed it. Incremental friction reduction within the existing model wasn't enough – the model itself was the problem.

I proposed removing the security key requirement entirely and reframing enrollment as a progressive, flexible journey – a building-block model where each step independently strengthened a user's account security, so even if someone dropped off partway through, they'd be better protected than when they started. This was controversial: it meant accepting some phishability in exchange for dramatically broader coverage. But the alternative was worse – protecting a small number of users perfectly while leaving the vast majority of high-risk users exposed.

I built the case, presented it to L8 leadership, and initiated a three-day cross-functional workshop spanning four time zones (Tel Aviv, Zurich, Sunnyvale, New York) to pressure-test the direction and align the team. The discussions directly shaped the 2022 product roadmap. No one asked me to organize this – I saw a strategic gap and filled it.

Shipped Q1 2022

Security Key Wizard

An educational registration flow with type-specific visual animations, designed to be modular. Reused across four identity flows and featured on Google's external Security Blog.

While reviewing a colleague's 2SV settings revamp, I spotted an opportunity nobody had scoped: users didn't understand security keys. UXR had found two distinct comprehension gaps – users couldn't articulate the benefit of using keys over the friction they caused, and they couldn't explain how signing in with a key actually worked. Some thought the key would replace their password entirely. This was the program's most persistent pain point, and no one had owned a solution.

I designed a guided wizard that addressed both gaps. At registration, the wizard introduced a key type chooser – showing the user's phone-based built-in keys alongside physical options – then after registration, provided contextual educational steps with animations specific to the type of key just registered. If you added a USB key with NFC, you'd see NFC sign-in instructions first, then USB. If you added a phone, you'd see the phone-based flow. Users could also access a self-guided "How to use security keys" education page before starting, with illustrations for all four key types.

The wizard had immediate impact on Advanced Protection by enabling phone-as-a-security-key enrollment, reducing the requirement from two physical keys to one. For users with 2+ registered keys, the wizard also surfaced an Advanced Protection enrollment prompt – connecting the 2SV registration flow directly to the program. The educational components were adopted across the 2SV settings and Advanced Protection enrollment flows, informed Google's broader Secure-by-Default educational strategy, and were featured on Google's external Security Blog. This cross-flow reuse was intentional: the identity and security space at Google had a fragmentation problem, and building shared components was one way to address it.

Shipped Q1 2022

Advanced Protection Settings page

The program's first dedicated settings page in its five-year history, giving enrolled users a home to manage their protections.

Advanced Protection had existed since 2017 without a dedicated place for enrolled users to manage their protections. Everything was buried alongside general 2SV settings – users couldn't tell which settings belonged to the program and which were standard account security. Nobody had flagged this as a problem; it had just always been that way.

I identified the gap, defined the scope as a leaf page leveraging existing MyAccount patterns, and aligned the Advanced Protection and 2SV teams to prevent feature duplication between the two settings surfaces. The page gave enrolled users a home for the first time – a place to see what the program was doing for them and manage their enrollment.

Shipped Q1 2022

Enhanced Safe Browsing in Google Account

Cross-functional initiative spanning Chrome, Safe Browsing, and Privacy Counsel. Projected ~40M newly opted-in users, >35% less likely to be phished.

Enhanced Safe Browsing was a Chrome-originated feature that needed to be integrated into the Google Account security surface – requiring alignment across Chrome, Safe Browsing, Privacy Counsel, and the Security Checkup team. The existing consent messaging was vague about what users were agreeing to, and nobody was pushing to fix it.

I advocated for user research on the consent language – the kind of initiative that's easy to skip when there's launch pressure. The findings fundamentally shaped the final consent approach, ensuring users understood what Enhanced Safe Browsing does before they opted in. The launch projected ~40 million newly opted-in users who would be >35% less likely to be phished, and received significant media coverage.

The principle I took from this work – that how you ask for consent shapes whether users trust the feature – became foundational to my OpenPass work at The Trade Desk, where I separated the consent screen from the email entry to reduce cognitive load and provide genuine transparency. Same design judgment, different identity context.

Q1 2022 | Enrollment proposal approved

After obtaining cross-functional alignment, the guided enrollment proposal was approved by Google Account Security's senior leadership – a groundbreaking accomplishment given that it challenged the program's founding premise of unphishable protection.

The full enrollment redesign didn't ship during my time on the team due to resourcing constraints. But the direction proved correct: Google's 2025 launch of passkey-based simplified enrollment realized the core thesis I'd championed – replacing the hardware key barrier with a more accessible authentication method so the program could protect more people. The specific implementation evolved (passkeys instead of PaaSK), but the design insight held: the enrollment barrier was the program's biggest problem, not a necessary tradeoff.

Shipped 2022–2023

Security Settings redesign

Led a UX initiative to redesign the Security tab for 4B users. The shipped P0 drove 25% increase in 2SV adoption and 40% lift in sign-in method diversification. The framework still governs the page today.

This wasn't on anyone's roadmap. Google was preparing to auto-enroll all 2SV-capable accounts as part of Secure by Default – meaning 4 billion users would soon land on a Security Settings page that couldn't clearly explain what they'd been enrolled into. The tab's core problem: users couldn't distinguish recovery methods from authentication methods, and the push toward device-first authentication was about to make that confusion worse.

I led the redesign initiative, which required alignment across four teams that didn't typically coordinate: Secure-by-Default, My Google, third-party access, and the Access page team. Each had their own roadmap and design conventions. I identified that the highest-leverage intervention was narrowing to the 2SV onboarding entry point and a suggestive chip pattern – directly supporting the upcoming auto-enrollment while establishing the framework the team could build on incrementally. This scoping decision also eliminated a planned interim 2SV settings update, reducing duplicated engineering effort across the Google Account Security team.

The framework I established – renaming "Security Settings" to "Security & sign-in," the "How you sign in to Google" consolidated section, the chip-based affordance for adding methods, and inline warning alerts – still shapes the page 4 billion users land on, three years later.

Looking ahead

Patterns that carried forward into OpenPass

The design principles I developed across these initiatives didn't stay at Google. When I joined The Trade Desk to lead OpenPass – a consumer authentication product for the open internet – I applied the same core judgments in a different identity context: separating consent from action to build genuine transparency, designing progressive flows where each step delivers value even if the user doesn't complete the journey, building for users who are trying to do something else and didn't come to think about identity, and creating reusable patterns across fragmented surfaces rather than solving one flow at a time.
